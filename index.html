“Explainable and Fair Credit Default Prediction Across Multiple Financial Datasets Using Machine Learning”

"Project Statement:"This project investigates whether an explainable and fairness-aware machine learning framework can accurately predict credit default across multiple financial datasets while reducing demographic bias and providing interpretable decision insights for risk assessment."

Problem Statement

Financial institutions rely heavily on credit risk prediction models to identify potential defaulters, yet many existing systems prioritize accuracy over transparency and fairness. Traditional statistical models fail to capture complex financial patterns, while modern machine learning models often behave as “black boxes,” making their decisions difficult to interpret and potentially biased toward certain demographic groups. This project addresses the need for a reliable, interpretable, and fairness-aware credit default prediction framework that can perform consistently across diverse financial datasets while supporting responsible and informed decision-making.

Literature Review

Existing research on credit risk prediction has primarily used statistical models like Logistic Regression for their simplicity and interpretability, and machine learning models such as Random Forest and XGBoost for improved predictive accuracy. Recent studies have introduced Explainable AI techniques like SHAP to interpret model decisions and identify key risk factors. Additionally, fairness-aware approaches have been explored to detect and reduce demographic bias in automated credit scoring. However, most prior work focuses on individual aspects such as accuracy, explainability, or fairness on single datasets, with limited efforts toward integrating all these components into one unified framework.

Research Gap

Most existing credit risk studies focus mainly on accuracy using a single dataset, with limited emphasis on interpretability, fairness, and real-world applicability. While some research explores explainability or fairness separately, few integrate them into one unified system or evaluate performance across multiple financial datasets. This project bridges that gap by combining predictive modeling, SHAP-based explainability, fairness analysis, and bias mitigation into a single framework, making the system more transparent, fair, and practical for responsible credit decision-making.

System Methodology

Dataset

Three publicly available credit risk datasets were used to ensure diverse and reliable evaluation: • Taiwan Credit Card (UCI) – 30,000 records with demographic, billing, and repayment history. • German Credit (UCI) – 1,000 financial and socio-economic profiles. • Give Me Some Credit (Kaggle) – ~150,000 borrower records with income, debt, and delinquency features. Feature engineering was applied (payment score, utilization ratio), followed by stratified splitting, scaling, and class imbalance handling using SMOTE and class weighting.

Model / Architecture

Multiple models were trained and compared: • Logistic Regression – baseline model • Random Forest – ensemble learning • XGBoost – best performing model Evaluation included Accuracy, Precision, Recall, F1-score, ROC-AUC, and 5-fold cross-validation. Threshold tuning improved risk sensitivity, while SHAP explainability and fairness-aware reweighting enhanced transparency and bias mitigation.

Results & Analysis

Best ROC-AUC Achieved 0.86

XGBoost emerged as the best-performing model across all datasets, achieving ROC-AUC scores of 0.77 (Taiwan), 0.80 (German), and 0.86 (GiveMeSomeCredit). Threshold optimization improved sensitivity toward high-risk borrowers, enabling better detection of potential defaulters. SHAP-based explainability identified key financial risk drivers, including repayment delays, credit utilization, and past-due behavior. Fairness-aware reweighting reduced demographic disparity while maintaining predictive performance and slightly lowering estimated financial risk.
